\chapter*{General Introduction}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{General Introduction}
\markboth{General Introduction}{General Introduction}
\label{chap:introduction}

Current neural networks achieve high accuracy at the expense of performing numerous computations with massive numbers of weights. This poses a deployment problem as we are faced with memory, latency, and power constraints in the environment where the model needs to run. These trade-offs between performance and cost are particularly transparent in Transformer models due to multiple repetitions of attention projections, feed-forward layers, residual connections, and normalization steps. In this report we will explore pruning as one approach to lower cost by pruning weights and entire Transformer components from a trained network.

In order to pose that problem in an accurate way, the report first defines some of the foundations of neural networks, Transformer architecture and the cost on hardware. This part presents the basic vocabulary which is used to express what can be discarded, what is modified in the remaining network and where the cost arises during execution. From that definition, pruning is presented as a constrained modification on a trained model, described by the concepts of density, sparsity, topology, and recovery.

With all that we've discussed in the previous chapters, we arrive at pruning, where we isolate the questions that a method needs to answer. What structure can be removed, when importance is given to that structure by a criterion, at what stage removal is carried out, and under which practical regime the method falls, are defined in the chapter, and then mapped back to the context of Transformers in the shape of attention heads, feed-forward channels and layers.


The report then reviews the main pruning state of the art methods through this structure and the empirical comparison reads their results under the same lens. The point is not to list methods one by one, together, they show the recurring issue
behind these methods: pruning decisions depend on local importance and on the way the compression budget is allocated across the model.
